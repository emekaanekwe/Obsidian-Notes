### Notes for Your Student: Percentages

| Conversion | Rule | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Percentage → Fraction | Write over 100 and simplify | $50\% = \frac{50}{100} = \frac{1}{2}$ |
| Fraction → Percentage | Multiply by 100 | $\frac{3}{20} \times 100 = 15\%$ |
| Decimal → Percentage | Multiply by 100 (move decimal two places right) | $0.561 \times 100 = 56.1\%$ |

**Strategy:** Always simplify fractions where possible. When converting a decimal to a percentage, remember that "percent" means "per hundred"—you are essentially scaling the decimal to be out of 100.

---

### Notes for Your Student: Percentage Word Problems

These three problems cover the three common percentage scenarios:

| Scenario | Key Operation | Shortcut |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Percentage increase | Add the percentage of the original | Multiply by $\frac{100 + \%}{100}$ |
| Percentage decrease | Subtract the percentage of the original | Multiply by $\frac{100 - \%}{100}$ |
| Finding the whole from a part | Divide the known amount by the percentage (as a decimal) | $\text{Total} = \frac{\text{part}}{\% \text{ as decimal}}$ |

**Strategy:** Read carefully to identify whether you are finding a new amount (increase/decrease) or finding the original total (given a part and its percentage). Drawing a simple diagram can help visualize the relationship.

### Notes for Your Student: Categorical Data

**Types of Data:**

| Type | Description | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Categorical** | Data that fits into categories | Colour, gender, activity level |
| **Nominal** | Categories with no order | Gender, favourite colour |
| **Ordinal** | Categories with a natural order | Activity level (low, medium, high), shoe size |
| **Numerical** | Data that consists of numbers | Height, weight, count of students |

**Frequency Table Strategy:**

1. Tally the occurrences of each category.
2. Count the total number of observations.
3. Calculate percentage frequency: $\frac{\text{frequency}}{\text{total}} \times 100\%$
4. Verify that percentages sum to $100\%$ (allowing for rounding).

**Mode:** The category with the highest frequency. A data set can have one mode, multiple modes, or no mode.

## Question 10: Chemistry Experiment

The results of a student's chemistry experiment were as follows:

$7.3,\ 8.3,\ 5.9,\ 7.4,\ 6.2,\ 7.4,\ 5.8,\ 6.1,\ 6.0$

**a.** i. Find the mean and the median of the results.

ii. Find the IQR and the standard deviation of the results.

**b.** Unfortunately, when the student was transcribing his results into his chemistry book, he made a small error and wrote:

$7.3,\ 8.3,\ 5.9,\ 7.4,\ 6.2,\ 7.4,\ 5.8,\ 6.1,\ 60$

i. Find the mean and the median of these results.

ii. Find the interquartile range and the standard deviation of these results.

---

### Notes for Your Student: Measures of Center and Spread

**Key Definitions:**

| Measure | Definition | Resistant to Outliers? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mean** | Sum of values divided by count | ❌ No (affected by outliers) |
| **Median** | Middle value when data is sorted | ✅ Yes |
| **IQR** | $Q_3 - Q_1$ (middle 50% of data) | ✅ Yes |
| **Standard Deviation** | Measure of spread around the mean | ❌ No (affected by outliers) |

**Strategies:**

1. **Mean of combined groups:** Always find the total sum and total count first. Never average the two means directly unless the group sizes are equal.

2. **Median:** Sort the data first. For odd $n$, median is the $\frac{n+1}{2}$th value. For even $n$, median is the average of the two middle values.

3. **IQR:** After sorting, find the median, then find the median of the lower half ($Q_1$) and upper half ($Q_3$). For odd $n$, exclude the overall median when finding $Q_1$ and $Q_3$.

4. **Effect of outliers:** Notice how a single extreme value (60) distorted the mean and standard deviation but left the median and IQR relatively stable. This is why the median and IQR are called **resistant** measures.
