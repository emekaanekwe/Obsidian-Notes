# ***Day (2023)***
# Academic Summary

**Article:** _Frameworks for Cultural Adaptation of Psychosocial Interventions: A Systematic Review with Narrative Synthesis_ by Sally Day and colleagues, published in _Dementia_ (2023).

## Overview

This article presents a **systematic review of frameworks used to culturally adapt psychosocial interventions**, particularly interventions originally developed in one cultural setting and later applied to different cultural groups. The authors argue that psychosocial interventions often lose effectiveness when transferred directly across cultures because differences in language, beliefs, values, social structures, and help-seeking behaviors can alter how participants experience and respond to treatment. Cultural adaptation seeks to preserve intervention effectiveness while increasing cultural relevance.

Although motivated by challenges in dementia care, the review examines cultural adaptation frameworks across a broad range of psychological and psychosocial interventions.

---

# Research Aim

The primary objective was to identify and synthesize existing **content-specific cultural adaptation frameworks**, focusing on:

1. What frameworks currently exist.
    
2. How they have been applied.
    
3. What intervention components they recommend adapting.
    
4. Commonalities and differences between frameworks.
    

---

# Theoretical Background

The review is grounded in the idea that evidence-based interventions are not culturally neutral.

The authors define cultural adaptation as:

>The systematic modification of an evidence-based intervention to account for language, culture, and context so that it aligns with the target population's values, meanings, and experiences.

The article distinguishes between:

### Surface Structure Adaptations

These involve visible changes such as:

- Translation of materials
    
- Culturally relevant images
    
- Appropriate language use
    
- Modifications to examples and scenarios
    

### Deep Structure Adaptations

These involve integration of:

- Cultural values
    
- Beliefs about illness
    
- Family systems
    
- Religious traditions
    
- Social norms
    
- Indigenous knowledge systems
    

Deep adaptations are intended to affect the intervention's mechanisms of engagement and effectiveness rather than merely its appearance.

---

# Major Theoretical Challenge:

## The Fidelity–Adaptation Dilemma

A central concept throughout the article is the **Fidelity–Adaptation Dilemma**.

Researchers face a tension between:

- Maintaining fidelity to the original evidence-based intervention.
    
- Modifying the intervention enough to make it culturally meaningful.
    

Too little adaptation can reduce relevance and engagement.

Too much adaptation risks changing the intervention's active ingredients and reducing effectiveness.

This dilemma emerged as one of the most important unresolved issues in the literature.

---

# Methodology

The study followed PRISMA systematic review guidelines and searched:

- PsycINFO
    
- Embase
    
- MEDLINE
    
- CINAHL
    
- Scopus
    
- Google Scholar
    
- Reference lists of included papers
    

Searches covered literature up to January 2023.

### Inclusion Criteria

Studies had to:

- Present a cultural adaptation framework.
    
- Focus on psychosocial interventions.
    
- Describe content-specific adaptation guidance.
    

The review excluded:

- Purely theoretical discussions
    
- Process-only adaptation models
    
- Assessment tool adaptation frameworks
    
- Technology-based intervention frameworks
    
- Framework applications that did not propose modifications to the original model.
    

### Final Sample

The authors identified:

- 2,034 records
    
- 38 full-text reviews
    
- 12 eligible cultural adaptation frameworks
    

published between 1995 and 2021.

---

# Key Findings

The review synthesized the frameworks into **five major adaptation domains**.

## 1. Content Adaptation

This was the only domain included in **all twelve frameworks**.

Content adaptations include:

### Language Changes

- Translation
    
- Simplification
    
- Culturally meaningful terminology
    

### Symbolic Changes

- Images
    
- Stories
    
- Metaphors
    
- Examples
    

### Conceptual Changes

- Aligning intervention principles with local values.
    
- Integrating culturally specific understandings of illness.
    
- Incorporating local explanations of psychological distress.
    

### Cultural Knowledge Integration

Some frameworks encouraged inclusion of:

- Indigenous healing practices
    
- Traditional beliefs
    
- Community wisdom
    
- Local cultural narratives
    

particularly when adapting interventions for Indigenous populations.

---

## 2. Context Adaptation

Eleven of the twelve frameworks emphasized adaptation of context.

Context includes:

### Setting

Examples:

- Community centers
    
- Religious institutions
    
- Homes
    
- Healthcare facilities
    

### Delivery Format

Examples:

- Group versus individual treatment
    
- Session length
    
- Number of sessions
    

### Personnel

Examples:

- Bicultural therapists
    
- Community health workers
    
- Non-specialist facilitators
    

### Population Characteristics

Adaptations consider:

- Migration experiences
    
- Racism
    
- Socioeconomic conditions
    
- Family obligations
    
- Collectivist versus individualist cultures
    
- Local healthcare infrastructure
    

The review highlights that intervention success often depends as much on context as on treatment content.

---

## 3. Fidelity

Eight frameworks explicitly addressed fidelity.

Two competing perspectives emerged:

### Perspective 1:

Retain Core Components

Adaptations should preserve the intervention's essential mechanisms.

### Perspective 2:

Retain Outcomes

Adaptations may alter core components if necessary, provided the intervention still produces equivalent therapeutic outcomes.

The authors note a major limitation:

Most frameworks do not clearly explain:

- How to identify core components.
    
- How much adaptation is acceptable.
    
- When an adapted intervention becomes a fundamentally new intervention.
    

---

## 4. Engagement

Nine frameworks highlighted engagement.

Engagement refers to the ability to:

- Reach target populations.
    
- Encourage participation.
    
- Promote retention.
    

Strategies include:

### Addressing Barriers

- Mental health stigma
    
- Cultural mistrust
    
- Transportation barriers
    
- Service accessibility
    

### Improving Access

- Orientation to treatment
    
- Community outreach
    
- Building trust
    

### Strengthening Therapeutic Relationships

The therapeutic alliance itself was viewed as culturally shaped and therefore requiring adaptation.

---

## 5. Cultural Competence

Six frameworks explicitly included practitioner cultural competence.

This domain includes:

### Cultural Knowledge

Understanding:

- Values
    
- Beliefs
    
- Social norms
    
- Historical experiences
    

### Cultural Humility

Practitioners must:

- Reflect on their assumptions.
    
- Recognize cultural limitations.
    
- Remain open to learning.
    

### Therapeutic Flexibility

Therapists should modify communication and intervention style according to cultural context.

The review emphasizes that adaptation involves not only modifying interventions but also modifying practitioner behavior and organizational systems.

---

# Most Important Conclusion

The review found that successful cultural adaptation is not simply about translating materials.

Effective adaptation requires coordinated modification across:

1. **Content**
    
2. **Context**
    
3. **Fidelity**
    
4. **Engagement**
    
5. **Cultural Competence**
    

These domains interact to determine whether an intervention remains both evidence-based and culturally meaningful.

---

# Critical Evaluation

The paper identifies several major gaps in the literature:

### Lack of Consensus About Fidelity

No framework clearly explains:

- What counts as a core intervention component.
    
- How core components should be identified.
    
- How much adaptation can occur before an intervention ceases to be the original intervention.
    

### Western Bias

Most frameworks were developed in the United States and for Western psychological interventions.

This raises questions about their applicability to:

- Indigenous communities
    
- Non-Western societies
    
- Low- and middle-income countries
    

without further modification.

### Limited Integration of Indigenous Knowledge

Only some frameworks explicitly incorporated:

- Traditional healing practices
    
- Indigenous epistemologies
    
- Community ownership
    

despite their importance for cultural legitimacy.

---

# Relevance to Psychology

From a psychological perspective, the paper reflects a broader movement away from assuming that psychological theories and interventions are universally applicable.

Instead, it supports a contextualized view of psychology in which:

- Mental health is culturally constructed.
    
- Therapeutic processes are culturally mediated.
    
- Intervention effectiveness depends on cultural fit.
    
- Evidence-based practice must be balanced with local meaning systems.
    

The review therefore contributes to **cross-cultural psychology**, **clinical psychology**, **community psychology**, **implementation science**, and **global mental health** by offering a synthesized framework for understanding how psychological interventions can be adapted while attempting to preserve effectiveness.

### One-Sentence Takeaway

The article concludes that culturally adapting psychosocial interventions requires systematic modification of intervention content, context, engagement strategies, and practitioner behavior while carefully managing fidelity to the intervention's active ingredients—a challenge for which current frameworks provide guidance but not a complete solution.

# ***Artnz (2021)***

# Academic Review

**Article:** _Towards a Formulated Model of Schema Modes: An International Schema Therapy Position Paper_ (2021), published in _Cognitive Therapy and Research_.

## Executive Summary

This paper is a **theoretical reformulation of Schema Therapy (ST)** rather than an experimental study. The authors argue that Schema Therapy has grown substantially since its original formulation by Jeffrey Young, particularly in its use of **schema modes**, but that the theoretical foundations underlying schema modes have become inconsistent and incomplete. As a result, the authors seek to develop a more systematic, comprehensive, and cross-culturally valid theory of schema modes.

The paper makes three major contributions:

1. Proposes **new fundamental psychological needs**.
    
2. Proposes **new Early Maladaptive Schemas (EMSs)**.
    
3. Reformulates how coping mechanisms generate schema modes.
    

---

# Background: Schema Therapy

Schema Therapy is an extension of cognitive-behavioral therapy designed particularly for personality disorders and chronic psychological problems.

The theory proposes that:

### Core Emotional Needs

Children possess innate emotional needs.

### Early Maladaptive Schemas (EMSs)

When these needs are chronically frustrated, children develop maladaptive mental representations about themselves and the world.

Examples include:

- Abandonment
    
- Defectiveness
    
- Emotional Deprivation
    
- Failure
    
- Mistrust
    

### Schema Modes

When schemas become activated, individuals enter temporary psychological states called **schema modes**, involving characteristic patterns of:

- Thinking
    
- Feeling
    
- Behavior
    

Examples include:

- Vulnerable Child
    
- Angry Child
    
- Punitive Parent
    
- Detached Protector
    

The article focuses on improving the theoretical explanation of how these modes arise.

---

# Central Problem

The authors argue that the existing schema mode framework suffers from several weaknesses:

### 1. Incomplete Emotional Needs Theory

Young proposed five core emotional needs.

The authors argue this list is incomplete.

### 2. Ambiguous Coping Theory

The traditional coping categories:

- Surrender
    
- Avoidance
    
- Overcompensation
    

are considered conceptually confusing.

### 3. Inconsistent Mode Taxonomy

Over time many schema modes have been proposed without a unifying derivation process.

The field therefore lacks a systematic framework capable of generating a comprehensive mode classification.

---

# Theoretical Foundation:

## Dweck's Seven Psychological Needs

A major contribution of the paper is integrating the motivational theory of Carol Dweck into Schema Therapy.

Dweck proposes seven psychological needs.

### Three Basic Needs

1. Acceptance
    
2. Predictability
    
3. Competence
    

These emerge earliest in development and form the foundation of later motivational systems.

### Four Compound Needs

From combinations of the basic needs emerge:

1. Trust
    
2. Control
    
3. Self-esteem / Status
    
4. Self-coherence
    

Self-coherence is especially important because it concerns:

- Identity
    
- Meaning
    
- Psychological integration
    

The need to experience oneself as a coherent person and the world as meaningful.

---

# Major Revision #1:

## Adding the Need for Self-Coherence

The authors argue that Young's original theory lacks a need corresponding to self-coherence.

### Proposed New Schemas

#### Lack of a Coherent Identity

The person experiences:

- Identity confusion
    
- Fragmentation
    
- Dissociation
    
- Unstable sense of self
    

#### Lack of a Meaningful World

The person experiences:

- Existential emptiness
    
- Alienation
    
- Disconnection from reality
    
- Meaninglessness
    

The authors suggest these schemas may be particularly relevant for:

- Borderline Personality Disorder
    
- Schizoid Personality Disorder
    
- Schizotypal Personality Disorder
    
- Dissociative Identity Disorder
    
- Chronic psychosis
    

because existing schemas do not adequately explain these conditions.

---

# Major Revision #2:

## Adding the Need for Fairness

The paper proposes another missing fundamental need:

### Fairness

The authors draw evidence from:

- Evolutionary psychology
    
- Developmental psychology
    
- Comparative animal research
    

showing that humans and other social animals react strongly to unfair treatment.

They argue fairness cannot be reduced to:

- Trust
    
- Predictability
    
- Safety
    

because unfair treatment can be predictable.

### Proposed New Schema:

#### Unfairness

This schema involves beliefs that:

- The world is fundamentally unjust.
    
- Others systematically exploit or mistreat you.
    
- Society fails to correct injustice.
    

Associated emotions include:

- Resentment
    
- Anger
    
- Powerlessness
    
- Victimization
    

The authors propose that chronic activation may produce a "Victimized Child" mode.

---

# Major Revision #3:

## Reformulating Coping

The paper's most important theoretical contribution concerns coping mechanisms.

Traditional Schema Therapy uses:

|Original Term|Meaning|
|---|---|
|Surrender|Accept schema as true|
|Avoidance|Escape schema activation|
|Overcompensation|Behave opposite to schema|

The authors argue these labels create confusion because they resemble biological threat responses such as fight, flight, and freeze.

They propose replacing them with:

|New Term|Meaning|
|---|---|
|Resignation|Believe the schema|
|Avoidance|Avoid the schema|
|Inversion|Believe the opposite of the schema|

The new labels emphasize that coping occurs primarily in response to **internal schema activation** rather than external threats.

---

# The Reformulated Schema Mode Model

The paper proposes the following sequence:

Trigger → EMS Activation → Coping Style → Schema Mode

Each mode contains:

### Cognitive Component

What the person believes.

### Emotional Component

What the person feels.

### Behavioral Component

What the person does.

Different coping responses generate different modes from the same schema.

---

# Example:

## Abandonment Schema

### Resignation

The person fully believes abandonment is occurring.

Result:

- Panic
    
- Fear
    
- Desperation
    

Mode:

- Abandoned Child
    

or

- Angry Child
    

### Avoidance

The person disconnects emotionally.

Mode:

- Detached Protector
    

### Inversion

The person convinces themselves they need nobody.

Mode:

- Hyper-Autonomous Mode
    

---

# Building a Comprehensive Taxonomy

The authors systematically combine:

- 21 EMSs
    
- 3 coping responses
    

yielding:

21 × 3 = 63 possible schema modes

before reduction.

After grouping overlapping categories, they propose a final taxonomy of approximately **40 dysfunctional schema modes**.

Examples include:

### Child Modes

- Disregarded Child
    
- Non-Autonomous Child
    
- Victimized Child
    
- Angry Child
    
- Enraged Child
    
- Spoiled Child
    

### Avoidance Modes

- Detached Protector
    
- Funny Protector
    
- Angry Protector
    
- Reassurance Seeker
    

### Inversion Modes

- Hyper-Autonomous
    
- Over-Humble
    
- Idealizer
    
- Pollyanna/Over-Optimist
    
- Daredevil
    
- Bully & Attack
    

---

# Scientific Strengths

## 1. Strong Theoretical Integration

The paper successfully connects Schema Therapy to broader motivational theories, particularly Dweck's framework.

This makes schema theory less isolated and more compatible with mainstream personality and developmental psychology.

## 2. Greater Explanatory Power

The new constructs:

- Self-coherence
    
- Meaning
    
- Fairness
    

allow the theory to address psychopathologies involving:

- Identity diffusion
    
- Dissociation
    
- Existential disturbance
    
- Chronic victimization
    

which were previously difficult to model.

## 3. Systematic Derivation

Instead of accumulating modes historically, the authors derive them from first principles.

This improves conceptual consistency.

---

# Scientific Limitations

## 1. Largely Theoretical

The paper is a position paper rather than an empirical study.

Most of the proposed additions remain hypotheses requiring validation.

## 2. Risk of Construct Proliferation

Adding:

- new needs,
    
- new schemas,
    
- new modes,
    

increases explanatory scope but also increases complexity.

A theory can become difficult to falsify if it contains too many constructs.

## 3. Potential Overlap

Some proposed concepts may overlap substantially with existing constructs:

- Self-coherence vs identity disturbance
    
- Unfairness vs mistrust
    
- Hyper-autonomy vs detachment
    

Further empirical work is needed to demonstrate discriminant validity.

## 4. Mode Inflation

Expanding from a small set of modes to roughly forty risks reducing clinical usability.

Clinicians may struggle to reliably distinguish among highly similar modes.

---

# Relevance to Clinical Psychology

This paper represents an attempt to transform Schema Therapy from a collection of clinically useful concepts into a more formal psychological theory.

Its importance lies less in immediate clinical practice and more in:

- theory building,
    
- construct refinement,
    
- cross-cultural validation,
    
- psychometric development.
    

The authors ultimately aim to create a new internationally validated Schema Mode Inventory and test the revised framework across more than 30 countries and 23 languages.

# Overall Evaluation

This is a sophisticated and ambitious theoretical paper. Its central contribution is the proposal that psychopathology can be understood as the interaction between **fundamental psychological needs, maladaptive schemas, and coping responses**, and that many existing problems in Schema Therapy arise because the theory's needs structure and coping taxonomy were incomplete.

From an academic perspective, the paper is strongest as a **conceptual integration and theory-development effort**. Its long-term significance depends on whether future empirical studies confirm that the newly proposed needs (self-coherence and fairness), schemas (identity, meaninglessness, unfairness), and coping categories (resignation and inversion) improve prediction, diagnosis, and treatment outcomes beyond the original Schema Therapy model.
